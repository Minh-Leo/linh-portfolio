All About Branding  

31 August 2020

This week is probably the one that I most expected in the whole program!!​

I love branding. I love talking about different brands. I love discussing branding techniques people implement for their businesses. I am also currently taking Brand Management as a major unit so the discussions we had here really add to some insights I have learned recently. ​

“A brand is not just a logo, a website, or your business card… It is an experience!” - Unknown​

“Your brand is what other people say about you when you are not in the room” - Jeff Bezos​

Just like these quotes, brand identity is one of the most important things when it comes to whether customers will engage with your products or not. The colors, the fonts, the motions, the messages, the website with customer experience design, everything adds up to the customers’ decisions.​

If the branding is right, we will successfully reach our desired target customers, and also be capable of delivering the value proposition that we had promised. Jezz Bezos’ quote just illustrates how creating good branding is already a selling point, especially through the word of mouth. There are several levels that add up to how a person perceives a brand, which can be broken down to different parts in this Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) model.​



In order to successfully build our brand, we need to distinguish these elements and build an association between them to create consistency in our communication channels.​

Regarding Happy Goodies, I want to link the brand to the true value propositions it delivers, that is the exciting, fun and happy elements in the products and service. Also this is a platform where some local businesses make their first online presence, therefore, the overall theme of brand imagery has to match to customers’ feelings and their expectations. This will also be a motivation for our customers to maintain their interests, and increase their intentions to purchase.​

I decided to use coral-clash-pink as a background of the website to represent a fun and easy vibe. This color was chosen to be Color of the Year 2019. The Pantone Color Institute explains that coral color ​“embraces us with warmth and nourishment to provide comfort and buoyancy in our continually shifting environment. It also emits the desired, familiar and energizing aspects of color found in nature”. ​Not only the website, this color will also be the one I use for packagingand postcards and other elements in the subscription and gift boxes as a way to show consistency in my brand. As the color represents fun and trendy vibes, I hope to gain attention from millennials and gen-Z who are likely to be exposed to new trends and updates. ​

The font I used for Happy Goodies is Unna - regular. This font is simple and easy to read, demonstrating the minimal style the brand is leaning towards. ​

According to the prototype, the site is easy to navigate and the steps to order the boxes are quick with only three steps, aiming to bring customers a fulfilling experience before, during and after purchasing. Linking back to the CBBE model above, the way the service illustrates resonance is by engaging with the community through social media, and other communication channels. Using social media channels can maximize how the brand looks and feels in the eyes of customers, and also support the brand in terms of customer engagement and relationships.​

In the workshop, we also talked and discussed the last two important elements of the business model, that is cost structure and revenue streams. Below is my decision on the cost and revenue in operation for now. Once launched, it is expected to fluctuate and depending on the situation I will update the status to meet demand. ​

Marketing & Sales

7 September 2020

This week Isaac talked a lot about customer journey and how we could improve their experiences from the first time they see your brand till the moment they actually take action. The concept can be illustrated through the AIDA model, which stands for Awareness, Interest, Decision, and Intention (Sales). There are many many ways to help this process go smoother and that includes creating a map that demonstrates specific stages that customers experience in their purchasing process. There are the 5 E’s that we should look into to understand customers' needs, how they make decisions and how satisfied they are with the journey. ​



Entice -​ How will our future customers first hear about us? What will trigger their interest?​

This indicates the level of coverage the brand is towards its customers. For Happy Goodies, the coverage will be fully online through social media, website, search engine and also through direct marketing such as word of mouth. The reason I develop both subscription boxes and gift boxes at the same time is because gift boxes tend to reach more people than subscription boxes. Imagine! You know about Happy Goodies because you had subscribed to our boxes before, then it's almost Christmas time and you think about buying a gift for a friend, you will then can choose and customize your box on the same platform you’ve been familiar with, how convenient! When your friends receive the gifts, they will now be aware of Happy Goodies. I also plan to implement a “Coming Soon” campaign around 1 month before launching to spread the news and increase potential customers’ curiosity. ​

Enter - ​What impression do we create for new customers when they walk into your shop/office/website?​

The website is easy to navigate and the steps to order are quick which only goes through 3 simple steps. The new arrival items will be in headline and lead customers through the purchasing process just like any e-commerce store, however add a little bit more of authenticity and friendly vibes to the slogans or brand mantra. The impression I want to create for the customers is fun, easy and simple, with a mission to help local businesses maintain their status.

Engage ​- Will customers make a quick purchase? Or should we build long-term customer relationships? How long do they stay on the website? Human-interaction or entirely automated?​

I want to build more creative ideas for the boxes, which point towards a specific group of customers e.g. gift for the introverts/ extroverts, gift for the sudden gratitude, gift for no reason, anime lover, etc. These ideas itself are unique and will attract more people coming to the store. I don’t aim to use much discount promotion as the store is already built with cost-driven structure. This process is entirely automated as other e-commerce stores, with a touch of personal interaction as the website should be set up to have a pop-up chat box when someone needs it.

Exit - ​What is our customers’ last impression?​

After the purchase, Happy Goodies will send customers confirmation emails with a link to track their parcels. It will also include a thank you card with customized notes to personalize customers’ experience.​

Extend - ​What triggers a repeat purchase? Do our customers ever come back, or tell their friends about us?​

We will follow-up after 1 week of customers receiving the package, and check if they feel satisfied with the products. Existing customers have opportunities to customize or build their own boxes through an interactive game on the website. ​

The Pitch
Are we ready, are we not? Just go for it!

14 September 2020​

Seriously I always had this thought that Demo Day is “someday in the future”, and then today Isaac said “Let’s talk about Pitching because Demo Day is in 2 weeks!”, and my heart just skipped a beat for that…​

Anyways…​

After lowkey whining about how soon it is going to be (in my head of course!), I started to freak out a little thinking about how much information, plans, and many twists and turns we had in the process of creating our first start-up idea. It was quite hard to imagine we can include everything in such short 5 minutes. I have had many presentations before, but usually takes 15 minutes and about a well-researched topic, not a whole business idea I built from scratch like 2 months ago.​


However, listening to Isaac talk about the pitch canvas and how to gather the information together, organize it, and translate it in the way other people can understand in a short time frame was really helpful and insightful. The canvas suggests that we should follow a particular topic order to make sense of our ideas. The order looks like this:​

1. Simple Statement​
2. The Pain/The Gain​
3. Product​
4. Product Demo​
5. What’s Unique​
6. Customer Traction​
7. Business Model​
8. Investment​
9. Team​
10. Statement/Call To Action​
11. Why You?​
As all other presentations, the opening and closure are the most two important things deciding whether the audience will listen to our ideas and act on the information we deliver. I decided to start with a problem statement, which hopefully can catch the audience's attention. Something like:​

“Hi bla-bla-bla, my name is bla-bla-bla. I’m here today to talk about Happy Goodies - a subscription & gift box service that brings local goodies to customers & helps local businesses make sales in COVID-19.”​

Short and sweet, the next one is extending and explaining the problem so that the audience can feel the pain that the community has to endure at the moment. Then, it is now a good time for the product introduction to enter to show the power and the improvement it brings to people’s lives. Next, I will dig deeper into how the product will work, what the target customers and potential partners are, how the prototype works. Then, introduce the business model, a general view of market competition, how we can differentiate ourselves from others, how much we need for investment and BOOM! That’s that PITCH! Easy peasy!​

Oh, something is wrong here, maybe it will be that quick and easy in a perfect world, or if I’m born with a pitching super power, but nope…*cry a little​

The process of getting this pitch flowing on the right path is a result of 2 whole weeks continuously drafting, reading, present mock-up, getting feedback, fixing, editing, present again, drafting here and there, again and again and again, until the speech is smooth, the time is met, and my multi-tasking skill is actually my super power now because of all the timing, speaking, moving my Prezi at the same time. ​

Following practice presenting for Isaac and Eleanor, here are some of the feedback I received for my 1st and 2nd drafts:​

● The colors used in the presentation should match with the colors of the brand​
● Ease out on the stats, ease in a bit more storytelling​
● Use diagram to explain business ideas (business cycles)​
● Never repeat what is in the slide, make use of all space and time​
● Show more of my thinking process and reasoning for my decisions​
● Value proposition should be separated for the end-customers and the business partners​
● Real-life examples​
● Mention tiny bit about how the business make money​
This feedback was exactly what I needed to improve my presentation. It is better for me to go straight to the points and lose all the unimportant information.​

2 October 2020​

Today is the Pitch Practice with all the teams and individuals. ​

This is an opportunity to present in front of all members of the program (virtual of course!) and gain feedback from them.​

After I presented my second last draft, I received some useful feedback from my peers. The key points were about how I should lose some of the moving elements in my powerpoint, because it may distract the audience to actually listen to what I have to say. I was honestly grateful that they gave me the comment because it means I could lose some multi-tasks during the pitch and concentrate on my content more.​

Apart from that, the ideas and the information I gave in the pitch was fulfilling and understandable which was thought to be successful if I ever launched. I was really touched hearing that from them and that was my motivation practicing more and more before pitch day comes on next Monday. ​
