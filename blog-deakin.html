Design Thinking Workshop
[SPARK Series] 

24 July 2020

This week at SPARK, we talked about how to use Design Thinking to strengthen our business ideas. By making the problem tree and filling in a value proposition canvas model, I gained some insights about how to take the next first steps following the write-down-my-ideas-into-a-notebook step. In the workshop, Issac introduced us to the 4 D’s of Design Thinking which are Discover, Define, Develop and Deliver.

● Discover: the process of looking at a problem from different angles, in order to gain an insight and understanding of a problem.
● Define: the process of narrowing down to the specific problem we want to solve (address one sub-category in a big picture/issue)
● Develop: the process of looking at all possible solutions, without any boundaries
● Deliver: the process of creating cheap, tangible, testable versions of solutions and asking for customers' point-of-views => market research for product improvement


(visit Issac’s blog at https://isaacjeffries.com/ )
It is definitely easier to break down an idea into smaller categories. I am still on my way to look into different problems and do some extensive research. However, recycling and reducing single-used waste has always been there in the back of my mind. That was the reason I decided to design a problem statement using the problem tree surrounding the ‘throwaway culture’ - retail products single-used packaging issue. This tree consists of 3 parts: problem (the trunk of the tree), cause (the root of the tree), and causes (the branch of the tree).

As we all know, food packaging provides a way to make food safe, reliable, clean and also a way for brands to conduct marketing strategies for their products. Unfortunately, most food packaging is designed to be used only once and only some can be recycled. Everytime we make that grocery trip to any retails and pick up some food, there is a huge possibility that we will buy something that is contained in a single-used package. I made a little experiment myself by taking a photo of all containers I had used once and thrown away and the result is more than horrifying. In a week, I had thrown away a total of 20 containers (in the form of boxes, cannes, plastics, glass, metal, paperboard, etc.) I, then, realized that if a hundred, a thousand, or hundred of thousand of people who did the same thing, the amount of waste every year (only from food packaging) is massive.

The negative impacts (the branches) of this ‘throwaway culture’ on the environment is countless. I only listed a few highlights here, and mostly are the damage to the ecosystem and natural resources. The causes of the issues (the roots) could be the lack of retail policy, or the ‘profits-focused’ issue, or that consumer mindset does take a long time to build habit. If we look at the plastic-bag ban, it did take quite a while to plan and execute.

There are already many zero-waste businesses out there who allow customers to bring their re-use containers to refill the products. However, those types of businesses are not widespread and retails like Coles, Woolworth, Aldi, etc. are still the main outlet to grocery shopping

By building this problem tree and digging deeper into the issue for the past week, I have learned a lot and on my way to form solid knowledge and figure out an up-to-date reliable information source.

I am more than excited to know that Woolworth, partner with TerraCycle, is launching a revolutionary ‘Loop’ platform in 2021 in Australian to tackle plastic waste. This platform allows Australian grocery shoppers to create a circular shopping system where finished product packages can be collected, cleaned, refilled and reused. Not only applied to food products, they also apply to other household products like detergent, shampoo, brand-specific products.

In 2021, I can start going to Woolworths for groceries without feeling guilty that something I buy will be the reason to destroy our living environment. This is a brilliant idea and I’m glad that Australian retailers are aware of the responsibility to protect the environment and are taking step-by-step to reach that zero-waste goal.

[Spark] The Lean Startup
[SPARK Series] 

28 July 2020

This week at SPARK, we revised about Problem Tree and Value Proposition as well as learned how to develop and generate ideas through “The Three Lenses” model and Lean Startup Principles.

In the workshop, Isaac talked about how innovations can only be successful if three conditions (the three lenses) are met: ​desirability​ (someone wants it), ​feasibility​ (we can deliver it), and ​viability (it makes economic sense). In order to evaluate whether they meet or potentially meet the conditions, we need to run our product through tests and use measurement to record performance.

As we discussed, I remember when I was in secondary school and had to make cupcakes for a bake sale at school. With my imagination, I did not want to make the same cupcakes as anybody else, so I came up with the ideas of making cupcakes with dark colour themes (black) and using local ingredients for new flavour instead of making bright colourful cupcakes with normal fruits. I was really proud of my idea and wanted to jump into baking right away. When I told my dad about it, he told me that he supported my ideas, however, he introduced me to the ‘demand and supply’ aspects and got me thinking about it. After that, although I, as a kid, wanted to keep my idea a secret until the bake sale, I talked with many of my friends about the idea and asked for their opinion. When they all seemed to be interested in that and would love to try new flavours, I decided to make the product and the sale was surprisingly successful.

As I remember the story, I realize that the simple principle as the three lense can make a big impact in the entrepreneurial process. In the story, the orders are Feasibility (I can make new kind of cupcakes), Viability (cheap local ingredients, bake sale as free venue), and Desirability (ask if my friends want it).

In the workshop, Isaac also suggested 2 books which are ​The Lean Startup​ by Eric Ries and ​The Mom Test​ by Rob Fitzpatrick. One of the quotes I really like and take away from the workshop is:

"We must learn what customers really want,
not what they say they want or what we think they should want."

Imagine if my friends told me they really like to try the new cupcakes but turns out they just said that to encourage me with the new ideas but didn’t really want to buy it, it would be a disaster. Well, I guess real life is not the same as secondary school, of course. However, that’s why there is a Feedback Loop: Build - Measure - Learn (Eric). This Loop perspective will help us continuously conduct different ideas and products until it is truly viable.
